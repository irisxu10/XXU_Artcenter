Digital materiality -- TouchDesigner

Project Description

This project uses TouchDesigner to explore digital materiality by generating 2D image textures, observing the similarities and differences between digital and physical artifacts. It focuses on four key aspects: Permutation, Intention, Complexity, and Control, while documenting observations throughout the creation process

My Idea

In this project, I explored a wide variety of image textures, experimenting with different ways to generate and transform them. I also incorporated audio as an input at various stages to influence and animate the movement of the textures. Each time I created a basic exploration of shape and material movement, I would dive deeper into it, so each type often has more than one version.

Audio-Driven Shape Transformation

This project explores the use of audio input to drive the transformation and movement of 3D geometries in TouchDesigner. Starting with a basic sphere, I applied a noise function to distort its surface, then used real-time audio spectrum data to animate and influence its deformation over time.

Each component — from geometry creation, noise distortion, to audio analysis and control — was carefully linked to build a responsive system.

From Wireframe to Liquid Surface

Building on my previous audio-driven shape exploration, this new iteration adds dynamic surface textures, vivid color shifts, and richer visual layers. While the earlier version focused on wireframe deformation, the updated version introduces noise-based material distortion and fluid, organic motion, enhanced by vibrant purple tones.

Multiple noise layers were added to create more depth and detail in the movement. Final outputs were captured after additional transformations and compositing, allowing the audio to influence both geometry and texture flow.

Patterned Motion: Audio-Responsive Arrays

Continuing the exploration of audio-driven deformation, this version expands into patterned repetition. A single spiked structure, shaped by noise distortion and audio input, is tiled across the canvas to create a synchronized array. The combination of sharp forms, vibrant purple gradients, and rhythmic motion transforms the piece into a breathing, reactive field.

By introducing tiling and maintaining real-time audio control, the system highlights the beauty of synchronized patterns and dynamic symmetry.

Audio-Responsive Particle Fields

Learned by a point cloud tutorialhttps://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Mt2hwb5cngA, this piece extends the original idea by integrating real-time audio-driven noise distortion. Each particle responds subtly to sound frequencies, creating an evolving symmetrical field that breathes and flows with the music.

Beyond the basic setup, additional transformations and noise layers were customized to enrich the motion, making the entire system more dynamic and alive.

Deep Field: Audio-Synchronized Background Particles

Building on the previous particle system, this version introduces a secondary layer of background particles. Reacting to the rhythm of the music, these particles subtly converge toward the center, creating a breathing, gravitational effect that deepens the spatial experience.

By layering multiple reactive fields, the piece achieves a richer dynamic symmetry and a stronger sense of immersive motion driven entirely by sound. The full video runs for 3 minutes and 19 seconds, set to the track "Mystic State & Elias - XOYO."

Displaced Memories: Audio-Reactive Cat Form

Expanding on a classroom exercise originally based on simple displacement techniques, this project introduces a playful twist by replacing the original banana image with a cat figure. The cat's form is dynamically distorted by real-time audio input, while layered gradients and circular compositions build a dreamy, rhythmic background.

The piece transforms technical practice into a lively character-driven exploration, where sound sculpts both shape and atmosphere.

OIIA Cat: Sound-Driven Softness

Inspired by the recent viral "OIIA Cat," this evolution of the cat displacement project focuses on enhancing fluid motion and softness. The cat reacts to audio input, creating a lively yet dreamy figure that pulses with the rhythm.

Background patterns and texture layers were refined to amplify the floating, breathing quality, blending sound and motion into a cohesive character animation.

A Friend's Cat

This project builds on the previous cat series with several key changes:

  • New material: Replaced the standard image with a real photo of a friend's cat, adding more personal connection.
  • Scale adjustment: Enlarged the cat and centered it to make it the visual focus.
  • Background change: Added a striped background that flashes according to the music rhythm.
  • Motion refinement: Made the cat's deformation softer to preserve its original cuteness.
  • Technical update: Introduced an LFO to control the background’s flashing and subtle movement.
  • Atmosphere: Overall, the animation feels more intimate, lively, and warm compared to the previous, more mystical version.

Resonance Fields: Sound-Woven Geometry

This project explores abstract form through sound-responsive geometry:

  • Base structure: Repeating circles transformed and layered to form double-helix-like fields.
  • Motion: Audio input dynamically controls rotation, scaling, and distortion of the shapes.
  • Visual effect: Semi-transparent purple layers create deep, flowing textures across space.
  • Atmosphere: The entire field pulses and breathes with the rhythm, turning sound into spatial movement.

Rotating Arrays: Audio-Driven Minimalism

This project explores sound-responsive grid systems through geometric repetition:

  • Base structure: Simple circle shapes cut into slices using gradients.
  • Arrangement: Organized into a uniform grid across the canvas.
  • Motion: Each slice rotates subtly in response to audio spectrum data.
  • Visual style: Cool-toned, clean, and minimalistic, emphasizing structure and rhythm.

Living Lattice: Audio-Driven Point Fields

This project explores complex deformation and particle-based visualization:

  • Base structure: Combined sphere and twisted tube geometries, heavily deformed by noise and twist operations.
  • Data conversion: Surface points converted into CHOP data streams to drive particle placement.
  • Audio integration: Dynamically modulates particle movement and density.
  • Visual style: Minimal black-and-white palette emphasizes organic motion within a digital lattice.

Golden Lattice: Evolved Particle Dynamics

This project advances the living lattice exploration with enhanced materiality and depth:

  • Material change: Shifted from white particles to a golden tone for stronger visual presence.
  • Lighting and rendering: Added real-time lighting to create depth and highlight particle textures.
  • Motion refinement: Smoothed audio-driven movement with stretched and normalized spectrum data.
  • Atmosphere: The lattice breathes and flows like a living energy field, responding naturally to sound.

First Steps: Learning Audio-Driven Deformation

This project marks an important starting point in my exploration of audio-reactive design. Using a simple torus wireframe as the base, I experimented with mapping audio spectrum data to geometry deformation, learning how to control movement through nodes like Analyze, Select, and Math. Although visually minimal, the process of refining audio-driven motion through trial and error became a crucial foundation for all the more complex works that followed.
